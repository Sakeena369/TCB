Catching Stories: Dr. George Lekha tells us all



Dr. George Lekha, is the ninth Principal of Mount Carmel College, Autonomous. Propelled with a need to give back, this year marks her thirty-second year being a part of the college. In this riveting conversation with Sravya Satpathy, she talks about what lies ahead in the future of MCC and what she brings to the table.







Q) You have been a part of the college for a long time now, so what are some changes relative to MCC that have taken place in your time here?

R) There are a lot. Increasing in population to 8500 students now, getting autonomy, adopting the semester system, and now with NEP, the institution has definitely grown. Our college's success has also contributed to the fact that we went from 23 to 70 courses, introduced PG programs, been recognised as a research centre, and introduced sustainability as part of our daily lives. These are some of the structural changes that took place. On a personal note, I find establishing rapport with the students is more difficult now. It was easier to do so back when we had all the time in the world. But, being a mentor has always been more important to me compared to academics.

Q) Thirty-two years is a very long commitment to make. With your credentials, anyone would have loved to have you as a part of their institution – so why Mount Carmel?

R) What I am today, I owe it all to MCC. I was a different person as a 23-year old here in 1991. My confidence is credited to this college. I want to pay her back with my best with all my years before retirement. And, I have an excellent team to help with that!

Q) Your profile in the official website of Mount Carmel mentions that one of your core driving principles is a propagation of Aristotle. How are you planning on inculcating this into a large demographic like that of Mount Carmel’s?

R) Collaborating and working as a group is what I stress on, especially for the PG students. The college, as a whole, is definitely not going to be a walk in the park, but if any concerns are brought to me, I will make it a point to sensitize as it matters more to me.

Q) What would you say is your biggest hurdle turned into accomplishment?

Student encouragement and their love. My first batch of ‘91 was a struggle but I pushed through with my students cheering. The lack of age gap meant that they could understand my perspective quite well. I’m still in contact with them and I can confidently say they are the happiest bunch I have taught.

Q) Do you have a favourite campus tradition that you look forward to every year?

R) Obviously, Cul-ah but Freshers week is also a close favorite as we get to identify new talent.

Q) What is the most memorable prank pulled off by the students that made you chuckle?

R) I remember hostelites coming into class with hundreds of bindis on their face and carrying books in buckets.

Q) Would you rather have a rewind or pause button on your life?

R) Rewind definitely, the younger days were that good. There’s this satisfying feeling that comes after a well prepared lecture. Nothing can top that.

Q) If you weren’t in this line of work, what career would you want to pursue?

R) I am very content with my profession because I feel it keeps you young. But in the younger days, I wanted to do medicine and become a doctor.
