One vote, three new governments

If Karnataka moves forward in hope post the 2023 state elections, Mount Carmel College is not one to lag behind. Hopping on to the hope bandwagon, just with three times the intensity, Mount Carmel welcomes three new governments with hope- the state government with Congress holding the reins, the new board of directors at the crux of all college matters and the selected rather than elected student council. Albeit, all their election/selection processes broke the conventions of ordinary, hope in the first few days of governance is a privilege the people are offering their leaders.

All the governments have rightfully broken stagnant, but valued, trends that preceded them even before they received the baton of power. With those changes comes the hope of a new era, the start of a fresh day without baggage from the previous one. Even so, trends might have been broken but we must look to see if breaking the fourth wall is in order too. Will the hopes of the people with one vote and three governments be catered to or will the adjustment period to these new changes stagnate real progress?


-Aamina Azhar


 

The Battle for the Ideal Nest Away from the Nest

You spend most of your school days imagining how it feels to move out of your home and make your own choices. With the same dreams, we seek places to live after we move out of the comfort of our homes, only for them to be crushed when we consider the options available and the challenges of finding an accessible place to live off-campus.

Rents in cities have risen by about 40% in the past year, making it difficult for students and other working professionals to find a place to live. While bachelors struggle to locate good residences within their budget, paying-guest housing has also experienced between a 25 to 40 percent increase in rentals. The Bengaluru bench of the ‘Authority for Advance Ruling’ stated they intend to impose 12 percent GST on PG and student housing for rates less than Rs 1,000 per night.

While PGs/hostels appear as a more viable choice when you first move out, they come with their own set of constraints. When you relocate from your house to a tight space with little regard for boundaries, adapting to changes takes time and that takes a toll on your mental health. Living with roommates who have different opinions and do not share your regard for hygiene is also an inconvenience. The primary issue with PGs is the bland and repetitive meals, which lead to eating out, which just adds to student expenditure. While apartments may appear to be a more liberating option, they come with significant commitments.

Every place, whether PG or apartment, has its own set of advantages and disadvantages, but relocating to a new location is a transition that each of us must acclimate to on our own time. No location will ever meet all of your expectations when you move in, but you work with the resources you have and build a place to call as your new home.


-Avni S Shetty


 

Bodies as a site of structural violence and in turn, protest

Women have been caught up in a relentless amount of body politics for decades, and have reached the point of unfazed composure when presented with the violence in the world against women. The question that can’t help but be voiced is how much closer have we really brought women to a safer reality?

Throughout history, structural violence against women has persisted as a devastating weapon in times of war. Prominent examples, such as the 2002 Gujarat riots and the recent Manipur incident, demonstrate how sexual violence has been employed during communal/ethnic clashes. Using women's bodies as tools to further political goals has been dismissed as mere "collateral damage," denying them the recognition and respect they rightly deserve for their unintended involvement in these conflicts.

The act of rape continues to be used to control, degrade, and manipulate women. To establish their authority, men from various communities may engage in the violation of women, thus contributing to the extensive and all-encompassing prevalence of this issue. Patriarchy and entitlement of men are so deeply rooted in society that many men arrogantly believe that laying a hand upon a woman lies well within their “right” as a man.

The link between violence and lower caste only adds to the complexity of this issue. People have often expressed how in the social hierarchy, the men have it good, the women have it bad and the lower caste women have it worse.

Unfortunately, society and the government have both shown that they are unable to adequately protect the rights of women in the nation. In order to ensure the safety, dignity, and equality of all women, regardless of their background, it is critical for us as a culture to confront these ingrained issues.


-Khushi Jaiswal, Samhita Vasisht
